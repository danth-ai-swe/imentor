In level term life insurance (Term life insurance that provides a policy

benefit—and usually the premium—remain the same over the policy term.

Decreasing Term Insurance
In decreasing term life insurance (Term life insurance that provides a policy
benefit that decreases in amount over the term of coverage.), the policy
benefit decreases at stated intervals, but the premium amount usually
remains the same. Decreasing term life is popular for people who have a
financial need that decreases in amount over a defined time.
A common form of decreasing term is mortgage insurance (A plan of
decreasing term insurance designed to provide a benefit amount that
corresponds to the decreasing amount owed on a mortgage loan. )—the
decreasing benefit corresponds to the decreasing balance on a mortgage
loan.

Other types of decreasing term life:
Credit life insurance - Pays the balance on relatively short-term loans (such
as automobile, education, home equity, and personal loans). The policy
benefit decreases as the loan balance does. Unlike mortgage insurance, credit
life insurance proceeds are always payable to the lender or creditor.
Family income coverage - Pays a stated monthly income benefit amount to
the beneficiary if the insured dies during the term of coverage. Monthly
benefits continue until the end of the term specified when the coverage was
purchased. The longer the insured lives, the shorter the monthly benefit
period and the smaller the benefit amount.

Return of Premium (ROP) Insurance
Return of premium (ROP) term insurance is a type of term coverage that

Pays a death benefit if the insured dies within the policy term

Returns all premiums paid on the policy if the insured is still alive at
the end of the policy term

Conversion Amount
Insurers limit the amount of converted cash value coverage to a percentage
of the original term policy's face amount.
Typically, the policyowner can convert the full face amount of term
coverage to cash value coverage within the first few years following policy
issue. However, as the end of the conversion period gets closer, the
convertible amount often decreases.
Convertible Term Premiums
Unlike premiums for renewed term coverage, premiums for converted
policies aren’t always based on the insured’s attained age.
Convertible term policies allow for either an attained age conversion (A
conversion of a term life insurance policy to a cash value life insurance policy
in which the premium rate for the cash value policy is based on the insured's
age at the time the policy is converted. Contrast with original age
conversion) or an original age conversion (A conversion of a term life
insurance policy to a cash value life insurance policy in which the premium
rate for the cash value policy is based on the insured's age when the original

term life insurance policy was issued. Contrast with attained age
conversion.), which bases new premium rates on the insured’s age at the
time the original policy was issued.
This is attractive to customers because premiums are lower when the
insured is younger. Original age conversions do have some strings attached,
though...
Attained Age vs. Original Age Conversion
Example: Marcia and Todd, each age 30, purchased 30-year convertible
term life insurance policies. Both of them decided to convert their term
policies to cash value policies at age 40.
Marcia decided to convert her term policy on an attained age basis.
Attained Age Conversion
The effective date of cash value coverage is the date of conversion.
Original Age Conversion
Todd decided to convert his term policy on an original age basis.
The effective date of cash value coverage is the term policy purchase date.

The renewal and conversion privileges can lead to some antiselection;
insureds in poor health will be more likely to renew or convert their policies
because they may not be able to obtain other life insurance coverage.
To account for the risk of antiselection, insurers charge higher premiums for
renewable or convertible term policies than for comparable term policies
that aren’t renewable or convertible.
