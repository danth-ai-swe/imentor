Solvency vs. Insolvency
A top concern for insurance regulators is an insurer's ability to pay its
obligations to customers as they come due.
Generally, regulators want to help insurers avoid insolvency, a situation in
which the business is unable to meet its financial obligations on time. The
opposite of insolvency is solvency—the ability to pay financial obligations on
time.

Two situations can cause an insurer to be insolvent. First, the insurer might
not have enough assets relative to its liabilities.

Assets? Think "things of value"—like cash, financial securities,
buildings, and land.

Liabilities? Think "debts and obligations," including amounts that will
be payable to customers.
Second, even though the insurer seems to have enough assets to cover its
liabilities, the insurer might not have enough CASH to pay the amounts it
owes NOW.
Imagine that a large number of policyowners surrender their policies at one
time. The insurer might have enough assets but not enough "liquid" assets.
Liquid assets? Cash or other assets that are readily marketable for their
underlying value.
Not all assets are liquid. Suppose an insurer owns a skyscraper valued at
hundreds of millions of dollars. It can take months or years to find a buyer
who is willing and able to buy the building and to complete the sale. Real
estate is NOT a liquid asset.

Profit vs. Profitability
Net income or net loss, as we saw on the income statement, is the
difference between revenue and expenses during a particular time period—
such as a month, a quarter, or a year. So, the income statement measures
financial success (or failure) during a relatively short period of time.
Profitability is a broader measure of financial success that stretches across
many accounting periods of profit and/or loss.
Profitability refers to a company's ability to generate profit and its ability
to increase the wealth—or value—of the business over time. Steady, long-
term profitability results in a steady increase in the insurer's owners'
equity—its "capital."

Capital Management and Risk
Capital management involves high-level, strategic decisions that require
input from the highest executives, including the CEO, CFO, and board of
directors.
So, when companies decide to deploy capital, they choose the option that
will provide the highest return on capital, right?
Not quite. Return is part of it. But there's another issue…
…Risk!
Recall from our ERM discussion that the insurer must consider not only the
potential return of the investment, but also the risk of the investment, and
whether the return justifies taking the risk.
Just because a capital investment is expected to earn its required rate of
return, doesn't mean that we'll make that investment. Our stakeholders
expect us to maintain a relatively low risk profile to safeguard our
customers' funds.
