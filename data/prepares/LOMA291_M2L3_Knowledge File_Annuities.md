Module 2: Serving the Customer throughout the
Policy Lifecycle
A customer's contact with an insurer most likely begins when they are
thinking about purchasing a policy, and can last until the policyowner
dies and the insurer pays the policy proceeds to the beneficiary. In
between, the insurer underwrites and issues the policy, and meets the
customer’s service needs while the policy is in force. In this module, you’ll
learn about the tasks an insurer performs at each stage of the policy
lifecycle.

What Is Customer Service?
Learning Objective
Recognize the characteristics of effective customer service, and identify an
insurer's various customers.
Providing high-quality customer service gives a company the opportunity to
outshine its competitors. Companies often sell similar products, so one way
they can stand out is by providing a superior customer experience (Cx).
Most insurers provide service through a customer contact center that
customers can get in touch with by a variety of means if they need to
communicate with the company. Customer service representatives (CSRs)
staff these centers and assist customers.
A good experience with the customer contact center can go a long way in
building customer loyalty. On the other hand, a bad experience can cause
the company to lose that customer, plus several more. In the age of social
media, bad news travels fast.

Conserving, Up-Selling, and Cross-Selling
CSRs may engage in conserving in-force policies and in up-selling or cross-
selling.
Up-selling is promoting a more powerful, more enhanced, or more
profitable product than the one a customer originally considers.
Cross-selling is identifying an existing customer's needs for additional
products while selling, or after selling, a primary product and then
promoting complementary products that, combined with the primary
product, provide a more complete solution.

Active Voice of the Customer Data
Active VOC data is feedback that the company has solicited from customers, such as
responses to survey questions regarding interactions with the company.
Passive Voice of the Customer Data
Passive VOC data is unsolicited customer behavior or feedback—information that

customers behave when they visit the company website.
Companies also analyze customer calls to the contact center to gather VOC data.

An insurer can evaluate service quality in terms of the number of customers

Customer Satisfaction Score (CSAT)
Customer Satisfaction Score (CSAT) is a measure of a customer's satisfaction
with the services received from a company, expressed as a percentage. For
example, CSAT may be the percentage of all customers who complete an
after-call survey who say they were completely satisfied with the service
they received. CSAT is measured using highly customized surveys designed to
help insurers identify their specific strengths and weaknesses.
Net Promoter Score (NPS)
Net Promoter Score specifically measures customer loyalty. Net Promoter
Score (NPS) asks customers to rank their likelihood of recommending a
company or product on a scale of 1–10 and then subtracts the percentage
of detractors (customers who ranked their likelihood of recommending as
0–6) from the percentage of promoters (customers who ranked their
likelihood of recommending as 9 or 10).
Benchmarking
Using relevant metrics, insurers measure any improvements to or deterioration in
service through benchmarking. Benchmarking is the process of
1. Identifying the best outcomes that other companies have achieved for an
activity or process
2. Determining what practices produced those outcomes
3. Implementing the best practices to equal or surpass the best outcomes
Benchmarking surveys are often conducted by independent third parties to give
insurers an idea of what other companies' goals are and how successful they are at
meeting those goals. In effect, benchmarking allows companies to improve service by
implementing processes and procedures that have already been developed and shown
to be effective.
