Group Insurance Basics
Individual insurance products insure one person or a single family. Group insurance
insures the members of a group (such as the employees of The Stetson Company)
under one contract—the master group insurance contract.
Many different types of insurance can be issued as group insurance.
For example, The Stetson Company provides its employees with group life insurance,
medical expense insurance, disability insurance, and dental insurance coverage.
For now, we’ll use Stetson’s group life insurance policy to show how group insurance
works. Many of the principles are similar for other group insurance products. We’ll start
with some basic terms.

Lawful Purpose
Individual insurance contracts meet the lawful purpose requirement when insurable
interest is present. In other words, if the policyowner or beneficiary will suffer a genuine
loss if the insured dies, the contract isn’t a wagering agreement and is lawful.
Group insurance contracts have no insurable interest requirement.
Instead, a group contract meets the lawful purpose requirement because the
policyholder buys the coverage for others and is unlikely to benefit from the death of the
insureds. As a result, the group policyholder’s interest in the contract doesn’t present
the same risk of creating a wagering agreement.

Group insurance can insure a number of people at a relatively low cost compared to the

Sales
An insurer pays a sales commission for each individual life policy it issues.
A single group insurance policy covers many people, so its sales costs per $1,000 of life

Underwriting and Policy Issue
Group underwriting costs are lower because the insurer evaluates the risk
characteristics of the group as a whole—not the risk presented by each group member.
One exception is for very small groups. For these, underwriters will evaluate group
members individually.
Policy issue costs are lower because the insurer issues one master policy rather than
many individual policies.
Group Policy Administration
Group plan administration is mostly recordkeeping. Group life insurance requires
current information about

The name of each group insured

The amount of coverage for each group insured

The name of each group insured’s beneficiary
The group policyholder often collects and manages this information for the insurer;
whereas the insurer has to perform such tasks for individual insurance policies.
The more administrative duties the group policyholder handles, the lower the group
insurer’s administrative expenses.
We can categorize group insurance plans according to whether the insurer or the group
plan handles administrative and recordkeeping activities. The Stetson Company keeps
the records of each group insured and handles other administrative duties. This means

Participation Requirement
Group insurance underwriting guidelines require plans to cover at least a minimum
percentage of eligible group members.
The specific participation requirement depends on whether the plan is contributory or
noncontributory. When a plan is noncontributory (the policyholder pays 100% of
premium), 100% of eligible group members MUST BE covered. If all eligible group
members aren’t covered, the plan would be unfair to the group members who aren’t
covered.

Employees who believe themselves to be in good health sometimes choose not to
participate in a group insurance plan. Having too few participating employees means
that those who DID sign up are more likely to be in poor health. That’s antiselection.
If more employees sign up, there are more likely to be enough good risks (and enough
in premiums!) to balance things out.
