Through claims, a company fulfills its promise to policyowners and beneficiaries—
prompt payment of policy benefits when an insured event occurs.
Effective claim administration requires satisfying two somewhat contradictory
objectives.
1. Claims should be processed as rapidly as possible to meet the needs of
beneficiaries and comply with claims processing regulations.
2. Companies must ensure that they are paying only valid claims. A small number
of life insurance claims are submitted under erroneous interpretations of policy
provisions. An even smaller number of claims are submitted fraudulently.
Inaccurate or ineffective claims practices can cost an insurer financially and in
reputation. That's why companies establish standard procedures to ensure effective
claims processing. In this lesson, we discuss how a company reviews claim information
and determines whether benefits are payable.

Policy Status, Deceased's Identity, and Proof of Loss
Determining whether the policy was in force when the insured died—had
premiums been paid?—and whether the deceased was the insured are
typically straightforward.
Determining whether the loss occurred may be trickier. The claimant must
supply proof of loss. In the United States, an official death certificate is the
accepted proof of loss. But, a death certificate can't be issued if there's no
body.
So what happens if the body's missing?
When an insured disappears, the claimant is unable to present proof of the
insured's death. Whether or not the disappearance can be explained
generally determines how the claim analyst proceeds. Click or touch each
heading below to learn more.

Explainable Disappearance
The claim analyst may presume the insured to be dead if a specific peril can
reasonably explain the disappearance, such as a plane crash in the ocean
with no bodies found.
He probably would accept the claimant's statement and accident reports as
proof of death and would continue with the claim evaluation.
Unexplainable Disappearance
If no specific peril can reasonably explain the disappearance, the claim
analyst usually denies a life claim that is filed immediately after the
disappearance.
An insurer cannot approve a claim until it receives sufficient proof of the
insured's death.
In the United States, if the insured hasn't reappeared after a certain period
(five or seven years, depending on the jurisdiction), the claimant can
petition the court for an order presuming the insured's death.
A court order stating that the insured is presumed dead establishes proof of
the insured's death. If the policy has been kept in force during the
disappearance, the claim analyst usually will accept the court order as proof
of loss and will continue the claim evaluation.

The determination of whether accidental death benefits are payable can be challenging.
An accidental death benefit is a supplementary benefit under which the insurer pays
an amount of money in addition to the basic death benefit if the insured dies as a
result of an accident.
Example
The life insurance policy insuring Vinny Acworth's life included an accidental death
benefit. Mr. Acworth was hiking in the mountains along a high ledge and fell to his
death. If Mr. Acworth slipped and fell, his death would be accidental, and policy
proceeds would include the accidental death benefit. However, if Mr. Acworth's death
was the result of a suicide (he jumped), no accidental death benefit is payable.
When evaluating a claim involving an accidental death benefit, all facts of the case are
taken into consideration. The claim analyst may order autopsy reports, medical
records, and police reports to gather enough information to determine whether the
insurer is liable to pay accidental death benefits. If the benefits are payable, the insurer
adds that amount to the policy's face amount.

In most cases, the insurer pays the death benefits to the primary beneficiary or
beneficiaries named by the policyowner. If no primary beneficiary is alive at the time of
the insured's death, the insurer will pay the benefits to the contingent beneficiary, or
second contingent beneficiary if the contingent beneficiary is deceased. If a contingent
or second contingent beneficiary cannot be paid, the proceeds will be payable to the
policyowner. If the policyowner is deceased, then the proceeds will be payable to the
policyowner's estate.

The claim analyst will do whatever he can to ensure that he identifies the proper
payee, but there are instances when the beneficiary designation is ambiguous or two
payees seem to have rights to the proceeds. The insurer will encourage the parties
involved to resolve the issue, but occasionally, when the parties cannot agree, the
insurer will take legal action through a process known as interpleader. The insurer goes
to court, tells the court that it can't determine the proper recipient of the proceeds
through no fault of its own, and asks the court to determine who has rights to the
proceeds. Interpleader eliminates the risk that the insurer may have to pay proceeds
more than once.

Claim Investigations
If a claim analyst suspects material misrepresentation or fraud, the claim analyst
launches a claim investigation, which is the process of obtaining the additional

can be quite extensive. For example, in more complicated cases, a claim analyst might
call upon the special investigative unit (SIU), which is composed of representatives of
the claim, legal, and internal audit departments, as well as independent investigators.

Companies can use technology to automate existing tasks. Computers can
