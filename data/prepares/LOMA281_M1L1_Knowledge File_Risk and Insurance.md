Type of Risk
Learning Objective
Distinguish between speculative risk and pure risk.
What is Life Insurance?
Life insurance reduces the economic impact that occurs when someone dies.
How does it do this? To understand how first we need to discuss risk.
What is Risk?
Risk is the possibility of an unexpected result. This could be a gain or a loss.
Usually, we worry about unexpected losses, but you’d probably like to see
an unexpected gain, wouldn’t you?
Speculative Risk vs. Pure Risk
Some risks are speculative risks that can result in a loss, gain, or no
change...
…while other risks are pure risks that involve either a loss or no loss. There
is no possibility of gain.
Example of Speculative and Pure Risk
Example: Carol and John Wilson are both employed full time and their
family needs both of their incomes to support their standard of living.
Recently, they purchased $10,000 of stock in a growing software company.

===== Trang 6 / 31 =====

They believe that the software company will be successful and that their
investment will soon be worth a lot more than $10,000.
Analysis: The Wilsons’ investment in a software company is an example of
speculative risk because the value of their investment may increase, remain
the same, or decrease.
The possibility that one or both of the Wilsons could die leaving their family
with a loss of income is an example of a pure risk because there is no
possibility of a gain in that situation.

Life Insurance Terminology
Learning Objective
Define basic insurance terminology.
Insurance
Insurance is a method for transferring risk from an individual to an insurance
company.

===== Trang 9 / 31 =====

An insurer that accepts a risk makes a promise to pay an amount of money
(the policy benefit) if a certain loss occurs.
In exchange for this promise, the person buying the insurance pays an
amount of money, or premium, to the insurer.
What is Life Insurance?
So now you can probably guess what life insurance is:
A promise to pay money (a policy benefit) when the person named in the
policy dies.
For example, Barbara Allen bought a $500,000 insurance policy on her life
from Best Life Insurance Company. If Barbara dies while the policy is in
force, Best will pay $500,000 to Barbara’s husband, Bob.
Life insurance protects against the financial consequences of dying,
especially dying earlier than expected.
Financial consequences of death include the deceased person’s

Lost future income

Funeral expenses

Final medical expenses

Unpaid debts
Risk Pooling
Insurance is based on the concept of risk pooling (gộp rủi ro).
People who face the risk of a particular loss (tổn thất cụthể)—like the
financial loss associated with death—transfer this risk to an insurance
company.
Insurers collect premiums from all insured people and spread the cost (phân
tán chi phí) of the relatively few anticipated losses (sốlượng tổn thất dựkiến
tương đối) among all insureds.

What is an Annuity?
Like dying too soon, living too long—or longer than expected—carries a
financial risk.
Most of us don’t consider living a long life to be risky, but what would you do
if you ran out of money while you were still alive?
Annuities
This risk of outliving your assets is real. People can transfer this risk to an
insurer by purchasing an annuity and receiving a promise of periodic
payments for a set period or for life. Some annuities provide income for the
rest of a person’s life, no matter how long that is.
For example, Wladimir Dobrzynski might purchase an annuity that will
provide monthly payments of $1,000 to him for 20 years, with the
understanding that if he lives another 30 years, he will have to survive 10
years without that source of income. Or he might purchase an annuity that
provides monthly payments of $500 for the rest of his life, regardless of how
long he lives.

Contract of Indemnity vs. Valued Contract
Most insurance policies, other than life insurance policies, are contracts of
indemnity which base benefits on the actual amount of the financial loss
that results from a covered event when it occurs, subject to maximum
limits.
Most life insurance policies are valued contracts, which state the benefit
payable at the time of policy issue.
This benefit amount is the policy’s face amount (face value) because it is
listed on the face, or first, page of a life insurance policy.

Law of Large Numbers
How does a life insurer determine the loss rate?

===== Trang 19 / 31 =====

Insurers look at past events to predict the likelihood of future losses for a
given group. The larger the group, the more accurate the insurer’s loss rate
predictions are likely to be. This is true because of a concept called the law
of large numbers.
The law of large numbers states that, typically, the more times you observe
a particular event, the more likely it is that the observed results will
approximate the true probability that the event will occur.

Reinsurance
Reinsurance (tái bảo hiểm) is insurance for insurance companies.
Each insurer establishes a retention limit (giới hạn giữlại)—the maximum
amount of risk the insurer is willing to carry at its own risk. For example, a
life insurance company may determine that it will only insure up to
$500,000 on any one life.

===== Trang 20 / 31 =====

Reinsurance allows the insurer—the direct writer (công ty bảo hiểm gốc) or
ceding company (công ty chuyển nhượng)—to transfer (cede) risk above its
retention limit to a reinsurer (công ty tái bảo hiểm) or assuming company
(công ty nhận rủi ro). The reinsurer can accept all of the transferred risk, or
it can cede some of the risk to a second reinsurer, known as a
retrocessionaire (công ty tái bảo hiểm thứhai)

Risk Class
Not necessarily, but Seashore’s underwriters will carefully consider Hector’s
situation before assigning him to a risk class—a grouping of similar-risk
insureds.
Insurers use risk classes to decide how much to charge each person for
coverage. People in different risk classes are charged different premiums
based on the degree of risk they represent to the insurer.
Classifying Risks
If insurers charged everyone the same amount, lower-risk people would pay
too much for their coverage, and higher-risk people would pay too little.
By assigning proposed insureds to risk classes, the insurer charges a fair
amount for each policy.

Insurers classify proposed insureds whose likelihood of loss is not significantly

If a risk has all these characteristics AND if the underwriters approve the
application, is the risk then insurable?
Not quite. Underwriters have to verify that you need the coverage and can
afford to pay for it. They also need to verify that the policyowner will suffer
a genuine loss (tổn thất tài chính thật sự) if the insured person dies. We call
this having an insurable interest (quyền lợi được bảo hiểm) in the risk
insured.

Laws in most jurisdictions (khu vực pháp lý) require insurable interest to be
present at policy issue. This prevents people from buying insurance as a
wager (cá cược), hoping to profit when they have no risk of financial loss.
