Life Insurance Benefits
The main benefit provided by a life insurance policy is the death benefit
that is paid when an insured dies. Some life insurance policies also include a
surrender benefit. If a life insurance policy contains a savings element, the
policyowner may receive the accumulated value—the cash value—of the
policy as a surrender benefit.
In addition, a life insurance policy or an annuity contract may include
several other types of benefits that we will describe later in this course.
Actuaries must determine the cost for all of the benefits provided by a
policy.
Cost of Benefits
The cost of benefits equals all of the insurer’s potential benefit payments
multiplied by the expected probability that each benefit will be payable.
Cost of benefits = (potential benefit #1 × probability of payment) +
(potential benefit #2 × probability of payment) + …
Some products have only one benefit; other products may have many
different benefits. Regardless of the number of benefits in a product, dealing
in probabilities is where this stuff can get tricky for an insurer.

Compound Interest
Until a loan is repaid, the interest on a compound-interest loan continues
to accrue—or accumulate. In this situation, the investor usually earns
interest on both the principal and the accrued interest.
Calculating an interest amount on both the principal and the accrued
interest is called compounding.
Interest on both the principal and the accrued interest is called compound
interest.

Example
Marcia Woodson loaned Bernard Carter $1,000 at an annual interest rate
of 10 percent. Bernard did not repay any of the principal or interest on
the loan for two years.
Analysis
At the end of the first year, Bernard owed Marcia $1,100, calculated as
$1,000 principal + ($1,000 principal x 0.10) = $1,100
At the end of the second year, Bernard owed Marcia $110 in interest,
calculated as
$1,100 principal and accrued interest x 0.10 = $110
Thus, at the end of the second year, Bernard owed Marcia a total of
$1,210, calculated as
$1,100 + $110 = $1,210
The interest in our compound-interest example was compounded annually.
However, interest can be compounded with any frequency—quarterly,
monthly, or daily, for example.
