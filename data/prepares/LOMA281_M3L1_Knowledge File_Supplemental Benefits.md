Waiver of Premium for Disability Benefit
One of the most common supplemental benefits that may be added to
almost all individual life insurance policies is the waiver of premium for
disability (WP) benefit.

===== Trang 4 / 32 =====

Under a WP benefit, the insurance company pays the renewal premiums if
the insured becomes totally disabled and satisfies a three- to six-month
waiting period.
In other words, the insured must be totally disabled for three to six months
and continue to make the premium payments that come due before the
insurer will begin waiving premium payments. Some policies will refund the
premiums paid during the waiting period if the insured is still disabled
when the waiting period ends.
The WP benefit typically defines total disability for the insured as being
unable to perform the duties of
Any occupation
The insured’s own previous occupation

Any occupation for which the insured is reasonably suited by education,
training, or experience
Most WP benefits define total disability as the insured’s inability to perform
the duties of any occupation for which the insured is reasonably suited by
education, training, or experience. The insurer usually requires periodic
proof that the insured continues to be totally disabled.

Waiver of Premium for Payor Benefit
Most individual life insurance policies are issued to a policyowner who is also
the insured. However, sometimes the policyowner is not the insured but a
third-party. Under a waiver of premium for payor benefit, the insurer
waives renewal premiums if the policyowner, rather than the insured, dies
or becomes totally disabled.
For example, the benefit often is attached to a juvenile insurance policy
covering the life of a child. The policy is owned and paid for by an adult,
usually the child’s parent or legal guardian.

===== Trang 6 / 32 =====

insured child reaches a specified age, such as 18 or 21, when the ownership

Disability Income Benefit
One other disability benefit offered with some life insurance policies is a
disability income benefit.
If the insured becomes totally disabled, after a waiting period of three to six
months, the insurance company pays a monthly disability income. This
benefit typically contains a definition of total disability that is the same as
the definition in a waiver of premium (WP) for disability benefit.
The amount of the monthly income benefit is a stated percentage, often one
percent, of the policy’s face amount. Some DI benefits state either a
maximum monthly benefit amount payable or a maximum benefit period or
both.
Some disability income benefit riders also provide a benefit if the insured
becomes partially disabled.

Terminal Illness Benefit
The most common type of accelerated death benefit is the terminal illness
(TI) benefit, which pays a portion of the policy’s death benefit if the insured
has a terminal illness with a physician-certified life expectancy of less than
a specified period, usually 12 or 24 months.
Although TI benefits are commonly used to pay for medical care, living
expenses, or outstanding debts, they can be used for any purpose, such as
travel expenses.
