Module 2: Serving the Customer throughout the
Policy Lifecycle
A customer's contact with an insurer most likely begins when they are
thinking about purchasing a policy, and can last until the policyowner
dies and the insurer pays the policy proceeds to the beneficiary. In
between, the insurer underwrites and issues the policy, and meets the
customer’s service needs while the policy is in force. In this module, you’ll
learn about the tasks an insurer performs at each stage of the policy
lifecycle.

Best Interest
In addition to suitability requirements, insurers must comply with any best
interest regulations that apply. A best interest standard is a more stringent
standard than suitability. The state of New York has enacted New York
Regulation 187 that applies a best interest standard to all insurance
products issued in the state. Other states are considering enacting similar
regulations at the state level. The federal SEC has enacted Regulation Best
Interest (Reg BI) that applies a best interest standard to the sale of variable
insurance and annuity products.

===== Trang 12 / 72 =====

When Reg BI applies, the financial professional and the financial institution
with which the financial professional is affiliated must act in the best
interest of a retail customer at the time a sales recommendation is made,
without placing the financial or other interests of the financial professional
or the financial institution ahead of the interests of the retail customer.
Reg BI imposes four obligations:

Disclosure. The financial professional must make a full and fair
disclosure of all material facts at the time of an investment
recommendation. Disclosure includes facts about the recommendation,
any conflicts of interest associated with a recommendation, and the

===== Trang 13 / 72 =====

scope of the relationship between the retail customer and the financial
professional.

Care. The financial professional must act with reasonable diligence,
care, and skill to understand the potential risks, rewards, and costs
associated with a recommendation, and to consider those factors in
light of the customer's investment profile in making recommendations
that are in the customer's best interest, and that do not put the
financial professional's interests ahead of the customer's interest.
Although costs are expressly required in evaluating recommendations,
the financial professional is not required to recommend the lowest
cost option.

Conflict of Interest. The financial institution must establish, maintain,
and enforce policies and procedures reasonably designed to mitigate
(and in some cases, eliminate) identified conflicts of interest, such as
sales contests, that create incentives to make recommendations that
are not in the retail customer's best interest.

Compliance. The financial institution must establish, maintain, and
enforce written policies and procedures reasonably designed to achieve
compliance with Reg BI.
Insurers generally create procedures that must be followed to ensure
suitability or, when applicable, best interest. As part of the new business
process, the insurer's new business staff ensure that all the relevant
procedures have been followed. If the application is submitted by a financial
professional working for an intermediary, the intermediary organization
will generally also have procedures in place to ensure suitability or best
interest.

When a new insurance product is designed, actuaries make estimates about
a product's inflows, such as premiums paid and investment income earned,
and outflows, such as benefits paid and expenses incurred. For life insurance,

expected.

that is, if more people die—than the insurer anticipated when the product
was designed, then the inflows will be lower and the outflows will be higher
than expected. In other words, the premium rate the actuaries established
would not be sufficient to cover the benefits and expenses that must be paid

Antiselection, Persistency, and Insurable Interest
Three concepts that are important to sound life insurance underwriting are
antiselection, persistency, and insurable interest.
Antiselection is the tendency of people who are most likely to suffer a loss to
seek insurance. The possibility of antiselection makes the underwriter's role
particularly important in the risk assessment process.
Here, an expert discusses some red flags that underwriters look for to
detect antiselection.
Persistency is the retention of business that occurs when a policy remains in
force. For most life insurance policies, persistency depends upon the
continued payment of a policy's renewal premiums. When assessing the risk
posed by a proposed insured, underwriters must consider the likelihood that
the policy will remain in force if issued. To offset costs related to
underwriting and compensation of financial professionals, life insurance
policies need to remain in force for several years before they are profitable
to the insurer.
Insurable interest in life insurance is the requirement that a person seeking
insurance should stand to suffer a genuine loss if the insured dies. Insurance
is supposed to compensate for a financial loss if the insured dies—not
provide a financial windfall.
A person is presumed to have an insurable interest in their own life. In
addition, an insurable interest is presumed to exist between certain close

Teleunderwriting
An alternative to the applicant and financial professional completing a
lengthy insurance application is teleunderwriting, in which a home office
employee or a vendor gathers underwriting information during a telephone
or videochat interview. Often, the financial professional and the applicant
complete an abbreviated application and the other required underwriting
information is obtained during the teleunderwriting interview.
In teleunderwriting, the interviewer follows a prepared script of questions
and enters the answers into a computerized system. A business rules engine
may decide which questions need to be asked, based on the answers to the
prior questions. For example, if a proposed insured tells the interviewer that
she has a specific medical condition or takes certain prescription
medications, the system guides the interviewer to ask relevant follow-up
questions.
Insurers find that teleunderwriting often provides more complete and
thorough information than does an application that the applicant completes
with a financial professional because applicants feel more comfortable
providing personal information to a stranger over the phone.
Teleunderwriters are specially trained to ensure that all application
questions are answered completely. In addition, phone conversations can be
recorded and reviewed for quality assurance and to enhance an insurer's
compliance activities.

Premium Rates
To set the premium rate for a life insurance product, actuaries have to make
predictions about, among other factors,


To charge each policyowner an appropriate premium for the degree of risk in each

Classifying Risks
Once the underwriter has all the information she needs, she can accurately assess the
proposed insured.
Forthright uses a numerical rating system to calculate a proposed insured's total
mortality risk. A numerical rating system is a method of assigning a numerical value to
a proposed insured corresponding to the person's mortality risk.
The system has three basic steps:
1. Start with a base of 100 (standard mortality)
2. Assign a positive number (a debit) for each risk factor that has been determined
statistically to increase a person's mortality risk
3. Assign a negative number (a credit) for each risk factor that has been
determined statistically to have a favorable effect on mortality

Making the Underwriting Decision
If the application is approved as a preferred or standard risk, then new business
processing creates a policy record and arranges for any special services requested by
the applicant—such as an automatic premium payment plan. The policy is then issued

===== Trang 68 / 72 =====

and either mailed directly to the policyowner or sent to the financial professional to
deliver to the policyowner. The policy is in force when it is accepted by the policyowner.
If the proposed insured has certain medical, personal, or financial risk factors but is
still considered insurable, the application is rated: that is, approved, but at a higher
than average premium rate, or with a modified type or amount of coverage. For
example, a proposed insured with a history of diabetes could be approved for the
requested coverage as a substandard risk. The substandard-rated policy would require
a higher-than-standard premium rate. The term rated policy is generally used for a
policy approved at a substandard rate. (Some companies use the term "rated policy"
for any policy accepted at other than the standard rate—be it substandard or
preferred.)
When a policy is rated as a substandard risk, new business processing contacts the
financial professional who submitted the application and explains why the policy was
rated. If the applicant accepts the rated policy, then the policy is in force; if not, then
no policy is in force.
To modify coverage, the underwriter may approve the policy at the requested
premium rate but offer a lower amount of coverage than requested, or she may limit
the coverage by modifying the terms of the contract.
The underwriter can choose to decline the risk and not issue the policy.
