Primary and Contingent Beneficiaries
Beneficiaries are either primary beneficiaries or contingent beneficiaries.
Unless the primary beneficiary dies before the insured, the policy proceeds
will be paid to the primary beneficiary.
Contingent beneficiaries receive policy proceeds only if all primary
beneficiaries predecease the insured.

Revocable and Irrevocable Beneficiaries
Learning Objective
Distinguish between revocable and irrevocable beneficiaries and describe
restrictions imposed on beneficiary changes.
Revocable Beneficiary
Life insurance policies usually give the policyowner an unlimited right to
change the beneficiary designation at any time during the insured’s lifetime.
This right is known as the right of revocation.
Most beneficiaries have no right to the policy proceeds while the insured is
alive and are known as revocable beneficiaries.
However, in some cases, the right to change the beneficiary may be
limited…

===== Trang 14 / 34 =====

Irrevocable Beneficiary
Sometimes a policyowner makes an irrevocable beneficiary designation. In
that case, the policyowner usually can’t change the beneficiary or exercise
other ownership rights, such as take out a policy loan or surrender the
policy, without the beneficiary’s consent.
A revocable beneficiary has a ‘mere expectancy’ of receiving policy proceeds,
but an irrevocable beneficiary has a property right—a vested interest—in
the policy even while the insured is alive.
If an irrevocable beneficiary dies before the insured, then the beneficiary’s
vested interest ends and the policyowner can name a new beneficiary.
Community-Property States
In some states, laws limit a policyowner’s right to change a beneficiary
designation. One example is community-property state laws.
In a community-property state where spouses are entitled to an equal share
of each other’s income or property acquired during the marriage, a life
insurance policy acquired during the marriage is community property.

Premium Payment Modes
A policy’s premium payment mode is how often the policyowner pays
renewal premiums.
Insurers typically offer these modes:

Annually

Semiannually

Quarterly

Monthly
The applicant selects the mode when she applies for the policy and usually
can change it after the policy is in effect.
To keep administrative costs down, insurers typically require scheduled
renewal premiums to be at least a minimum amount. For example, Able
Life might require a minimum payment of at least $50 for a policyowner to
pay premiums monthly.
Insurers also usually charge more than a prorated portion of the annual
premium amount for more frequent premium payment modes, such as
monthly.
Example: If an annual premium payment is $2,400, the monthly amount
would be more than 1/12th of that ($200) to cover the additional
administrative expense.

Participating and Nonparticipating Policies
A life insurance policy may be participating or nonparticipating.
The owner of a participating policy can share in the insurance company’s
profitable operations by receiving a portion of its divisible surplus.

===== Trang 18 / 34 =====

Divisible surplus = the amount of an insurer’s earnings available for
distribution to owners of participating policies
The owner of a nonparticipating policy has no right to share in the insurer’s
divisible surplus.
What exactly is an insurer’s surplus?
Surplus is an accounting term. To understand it, you need to understand
the basic accounting equation:
Assets = Liabilities + Owners’ equity

Assets are all things of value owned by a company. Examples: cash
and investments.

Liabilities are a company’s debts and future obligations. For insurers,
the most important liabilities are policy reserves, which represent
estimated amounts needed to pay policy benefits as they come due.

Owners’ equity is the difference between assets and liabilities, and it
represents the owners’ financial interest in the company. For stock
insurers, owners’ equity is called capital and surplus. Capital is the
owners’ investment in the company and surplus is excess of assets over
liabilities and capital. For mutual insurers, who don’t issue stock,
owners’ equity consists only of its surplus.
Policy Dividend
Owners of participating policies can share only the portion of the surplus
designated as divisible surplus.

===== Trang 19 / 34 =====

An insurer shares its divisible surplus through policy dividends, which are
essentially premium refunds, not taxable income like stock dividends are.
Policy dividends aren’t guaranteed, but most insurers periodically pay
dividends on their participating life insurance policies that are expected to
remain in force over a long term.
Participating policies contain a policy provision that gives the policyowner
several choices or dividend options for how to use or receive a policy
dividend.
Policy Dividend Options
Mostly, the names of the policy dividend options describe each option well.

Cash dividend option--Insurer sends a check to the policyowner.

Premium reduction dividend option--Insurer applies a policy dividend
toward premium payments

Policy loan repayment dividend option--Insurer applies a policy
dividend toward the repayment of outstanding policy loan

Accumulation at interest dividend option--Insurer keeps a policy
dividend on deposit earning interest

Paid-up additional insurance dividend option--Policy dividend used to
purchase additional coverage of the same type as the basic policy

Additional term insurance dividend option--Policy dividend used as a
net single premium to purchase one-year term insurance
Three of these policy dividend options deserve a little more attention.

Settlement Options
The settlement options provision in a policy grants a policyowner or
beneficiary several choices or settlement options for receiving life insurance
policy proceeds.
The policyowner may select a settlement option for the beneficiary and can
make that choice revocable or irrevocable. Unless the policyowner chose an
irrevocable option, the beneficiary may select a settlement option when the
policy proceeds are payable.
Payees
The person or party who receives the policy proceeds under a settlement
option is the payee.
The party who elects an optional mode of settlement—either the
policyowner or the beneficiary—also can designate a contingent payee, or
successor payee, who will receive any proceeds still payable when the payee
dies.
Settlement Options
Settlement options are similar to annuity options.
Interest option
The insurer invests proceeds and pays the interest to the payee, typically
annually.

Fixed-period option
The insurer pays proceeds in equal installments for a specified period.
Fixed-amount option
The insurer pays proceeds in equal installments of a specified amount until
policy proceeds and interest are exhausted.
Life income option
The insurer pays proceeds in periodic installments over at least the payee’s
lifetime.
