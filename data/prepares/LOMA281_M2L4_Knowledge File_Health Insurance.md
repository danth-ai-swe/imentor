Forms of Health Insurance
Like life insurance and annuities, most health insurance products are
available on a group or individual basis.
Group Health Insurance
A group health insurance policy would insure the health of a specific group
of people, such as a group of employees.
Individual Health Insurance Policy
An individual would purchase an individual health insurance policy that
would insure the health of a named individual. Some individual policies also
insure the named person’s immediate family or a second insured person.
Health insurance protects against the risk of financial loss from illness or
injury and comes in three forms:

Medical expense coverage provides benefits to pay for treatment of
illnesses and injuries

Long-term care (LTC) coverage provides benefits for medical and
other services to insureds who need constant care at home or in a
qualified facility

Disability income coverage provides income replacement benefits to an
insured who is unable to work because of illness or injury
We'll look at medical expense coverage first.

Cost Participation Methods
Medical expense policies typically include features that require the insured to
share in the costs of some types of medical care.
Forms of cost sharing:

Deductible—a flat dollar amount of eligible expenses that the insured
pays before the insurance pays benefits.
Deductible example
Most major medical policies include a calendar-year deductible. For example,
a policy with a $500 calendar-year deductible requires that the insured
incur $500 of covered medical expenses in one calendar year before the
insurance policy will begin payments.

Coinsurance—a percentage of the allowable expenses remaining after
the insured has paid the deductible that must be paid by the insured.
Coinsurance example
Major medical policies often include a 20 to 30 percent coinsurance
requirement. For example, a policy with a $500 annual deductible and a
20% coinsurance requirement would require that the insured pay 20% of all
charges above $500.

===== Trang 5 / 33 =====

Most major medical policies limit the total amount of money the insured
must pay by including a maximum out-of-pocket provision, also known as a
stop-loss provision. For example, a policy with a $5,000 out-of-pocket
maximum will pay 100% of allowable medical expenses after the insured
has paid $5,000 to satisfy deductible and coinsurance requirements.

Copayments
Insureds under managed care plans generally must pay a network provider
a specified, fixed amount, known as a copayment, each time the plan
member receives services from the provider. For example, insureds may be
required to pay $20 for each doctor visit and for each prescription.
HMO members typically are only responsible for copayments. Other
managed care plans typically have a copayment for use of in-network
providers, and deductible and coinsurance requirements for out-of-network
providers.
Managed care plans place a greater emphasis on preventive care than do
traditional medical expense plans. Managed care plans usually cover
prenatal and well-baby care, routine physical exams, screening tests, and
childhood immunizations.
In addition, managed care plans cover many services that traditional
medical expense plans may not, such as:

Wellness programs (smoking cessation or weight or stress reduction)

Patient education programs that help plan members make informed
decisions about their health care

Long-Term Care (LTC) Coverage
Medical expense coverage is only part of the health insurance
picture…another part is long-term care (LTC) coverage, which provides
benefits for medical and other services to insureds who need care for an
extended period in their own homes or in a qualified facility.

===== Trang 16 / 33 =====

Because the costs of long-term care can deplete savings quickly, many
people turn to long-term care insurance to provide that care.
Benefit Triggers
A long-term care insurance policy requirement specifying the conditions
that establish an insured’s eligibility to receive long-term care benefits.
Two common benefit triggers are:

An inability to complete activities of daily living (ADLs)

Cognitive impairment
The activities of daily living (ADLs) are the activities of eating, bathing,
dressing, continence, toileting, and transferring into and out of a bed, chair,
or wheelchair.
A licensed health care practitioner must certify that the insured requires
assistance with at least the specified number of ADLs.
Cognitive impairment is a reduction in a person’s ability to think, reason, or
remember. Alzheimer’s disease and dementia are common types of cognitive
impairment.
Cognitive impairment severe enough to require regular supervision or
assistance to protect the insured and others around the insured triggers
LTC benefits. LTC policies generally treat severe cognitive impairments as a
benefit trigger even if the insured can perform all of the ADLs without
assistance.

Daily Benefit Amount
Long-term care insurance policies generally state a daily benefit amount,
which is the maximum amount of benefits payable for each day of an
insured’s long-term care at a care facility or in the patient’s home.
Daily benefit amounts typically range from $50 to $500. Many LTC policies
provide a lower daily benefit amount for certain types of facilities.
Example
For example, an LTC policy may provide a daily benefit amount of $200 a
day for care in a nursing home, $150 a day for care in an assisted living
facility, and $100 a day for home care.
Monthly Benefit Amount
Some newer long-term care policies provide a monthly benefit amount
instead of a daily benefit amount. Such policies allow the insured more
flexibility in paying expenses. Let’s look at a comparison of amounts paid
under daily and monthly benefits amount policies.

Elimination Period
Insurers specify an elimination period (waiting period) which is how long an
insured must be disabled before disability income benefit payments begin.
An elimination period reduces an insurer’s cost to process and pay claims
for conditions that last for a very short time.
